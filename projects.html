Generating synthetic data using AI | ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

Capstone project of the data science Bootcamp, Zurich, 2022. The project was proposed by the SIX group.

Companies nowadays handle large amounts of data and use it for many types of statistical analysis. In most cases, this data contains the personal information of many users. A solution to this problem is to generate synthetic data, i.e., data preserving the statistical properties of the real data, yet fake.

In this project, we generated synthetic data using two well-known algorithms in the field of deep learning: Generative Adversarial Networks and Variational Autoencoders. We also used not-so-known variations of these algorithms. Our conclusions agree with the current research. There is a trade-off between the usability and the privacy of the synthetic data.

Gravitational lensing and galaxy clusters | ASTRONOMY

Research collaboration with the Astrophysics Department of the University of Oviedo, 2020-2021. Remote.

Gravitational lensing is the effect of light distortion provoked by a gravitational force between the light source and the observer. The most prominent effect is the deformation in the shape of background galaxies, like the one captured by the Hubble telescope in the left image, where it created a horseshoe shape. This effect is known as strong lensing. The objects provoking such light distortions are known as lenses.

Another effect is the magnification of the light from more distant sources. This effect is known as magnification bias. This produces an increase or decrease in the detection probability of background sources near the position of a lense.

We explored the magnification bias provoked by roughly three thousand galaxy clusters from the SSDS (Sloan Digital Sky Survey). The background sources, nearly seventy thousand, were taken from the Herschel space observatory.

Positive Hopf plumbed links | MATHEMATICS

This is the topic of my doctoral thesis, University of Bern, Bern, 2017-2021.

A knot is a circle embedded in the 3-dimensional space, and a link is a collection of knots linked (or not) together. The simplest link is called a Hopf link: . In addition, if we give an orientation to the Hopf link, we can distinguish between two types of Hopf links: the positive Hopf link and its mirror, the negative Hopf link.

Two knots or links are equivalent when you can deform one into the other without the need to cut and glue. The video shows an example of how a knot with many crossings can be transformed into a much simpler knot (the trefoil knot).

Decomposition of the Kronecker product of two Jordan matrices | MATHEMATICS

Undergraduate research, Wayne State University, Detroit, 2013-2014.

A Jordan block is a square matrix of size \(n\) with \(\lambda\) in the diagonal, ones in the superdiagonal, and zeroes elsewhere, denoted by \(J_{n}(\lambda)\). A Jordan matrix is a diagonal matrix, where the diagonal is composed of Jordan blocks, usually denoted as \(J_{n_{1}}(\lambda_{1})\oplus\dots\oplus J_{n_{r}}(\lambda_{r})\).

We showed that the Kronecker product \(J_{2}(\lambda) \otimes J_{n}(\mu)\) is congruent to \(J_{n-1}(\lambda\mu)\oplus J_{n+1}(\lambda\mu)\) for characteristic different from two, and \(J_{n}(\lambda\mu)\oplus J_{n}(\lambda\mu)\) for characteristic equal to two. What about \(J_{3}(\lambda)\otimes J_{n}(\mu)\)? And for \(J_{m}(\lambda) \otimes J_{n}(\mu)\)?
